Jakkolo Plus

Team of 5

Semester 3 & 4

Since the university needed fully functioning and well‑designed projects for the open‑day exhibition, our team had to repair, complete, and improve an older prototype. The Jakkolo Plus is a mechatronic version of the traditional Jakkolo game, where players slide wooden pucks toward different scoring zones. When we took over the project, the electrical system was only partially completed and the wiring inside the control box was disorganized. We reworked the entire electrical box, replaced defective components, and upgraded several parts to improve reliability and user experience. Both the Raspberry Pi and Arduino programs were incomplete, so we refined the code, fixed functional issues, and added new features — including a sound module to make the game more engaging. Several mechanical components also needed repair or adjustment to restore smooth operation. Overall, we transformed the Jakkolo Plus from an unstable prototype into a polished, fully functional exhibit piece.

Automated Mehlwormfarm

Team of 6

Semester 5 & 6 - Trinatronics competition

For my final semester project, I participated in the Trinatronics competition, organized by TriRhenaTech and co‑funded by the Interreg program. Each year, engineering students from schools across the Upper Rhine region work together from January to June to develop a mechatronic system. Our team designed a concept for automating mealworm farming. Mealworms are a healthy source of protein, naturally consume organic waste, and produce excellent fertilizer, which made the project both sustainable and meaningful. While part of our team focused on marketing and convincing potential consumers of the benefits of eating mealworms—an effort that was surprisingly well‑received—the technical focus was on developing a functional prototype for automated mealworm cultivation. Using established knowledge from online sources—such as their sensitivity to light, vibration, humidity, and temperature—we designed a complex system consisting of several subsystems: a feeding unit, humidity and temperature control, a cycling system, and a web‑based monitoring interface. Although the prototype was far from market‑ready, we successfully tested each subsystem and integrated them into a well‑assembled final system. This experience was particularly special because we competed in an official setting and had to pitch both our concept and prototype to a public audience, which was both challenging and rewarding.
